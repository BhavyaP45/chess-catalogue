Chess Rules and Terms

The chess board 8 squares by 8 squares, for a total of 64 squares. The columns are lettered a-h (left to right), which are known as files. The rows are numbered 1-8, which are known as ranks. The numbers start from the white side, which makes it consistent between every chess game.

Both sides start on opposite ends of the board. Each side gets;

  • 2 Rooks
  • 2 Knights
  • 2 Opposite Coloured Bishops
  • 1 King
  • 1 Queen
  • 8 Pawns

A common rule of thumb when setting up the board manually is that the queens are on the square of their respective colours. In every game, white moves first.

Pawn

  • Absolute value of 1
  • Can move two squares from its initial position, but only one after that
  • It can only move forward in its file, but captures on a diagonal
  • If the pawn reaches the end of the board, it can be promoted to any other piece in the game
  • When a pawn moves two squares while an enemy pawn is guarding the first square, the enemy pawn can capture it. This is known as En Passant.

Knight

  • Absolute value of 3
  • Unpredictable, and can be used for many different tactics
  • Can move in an L shape, and can jump over other pieces

Bishop

  • Absolute value of 3
  • Can move in diagonals
  • Two bishops, each on a different color
  • Bishops cover large diagonals and can be vital for targetting weaknesses in the position

Rook

  • Absolute value of 5
  • Can move in both horizontal and vertical directions
  • Mainly used to control large areas and protect pieces from long distances

Queen

  • The second most valuable piece, with an absolute value of 9
  • Can move horizontally and vertically, as well as diagonally

King

  • The most valuable piece, as the entire game revolves around the king's safety
  • Infinite absolute value
  • Can move one square in any direction
  • The only piece that cannot be captured. Instead, when the king is attacked and cannot move out of danger, it is known as checkmate, concluding the game

There is one exception where the king can move two squares, which is known as castling. When the king and rook have not moved for the duration of the game and there is an empty space between them, the king can move two squares with the rook moving beside him. This can happen on the king's side, which is known as short castling, and on the queen's side as well, known as long castling. The main benefit of this is that it provides safety to the king and keeps him away from the most active place on the board; the center. In addition, it connects the two rooks, giving both of them protection from an external attack. However, castling cannot occur if the king has to travel across an enemy piece's line of sight, which would mean that the king is directly walking into a check.

There are several ways to draw in chess, with the most prominent being from both stalemate and the inability to checkmate (insufficient material). Stalemate occurs when one side has no more legal moves, such as when a lone king only has one possibility; to move into check. The next way to draw with your opponent is to receive a position where neither side can checkmate. This can happen in several ways, such as if there are only two kings on the board. These kings can never be side by side, and will thus never deliver a checkmate. In addition, a king and a bishop, a king with a knight and a king with two knights will result in a draw, as there is no forced mate. Another rule in chess is that if a piece is not captured in 50 moves, then the game will result in a draw.

With black to move, this position represents a stalemate, where the black king is not directly, yet cannot make any legal moves. This is a common mistake for people trying to checkmate with a king and rook.

Attacks

When you move to a square with an enemy piece on it, you capture it, which means the opponent's piece is removed from the board. You cannot capture with a piece that is pinned to the king, as moving so would give your king a check. In addition, the king cannot capture a piece that is protected by another, which is why it is important to keep your king safe to prevent checkmate.

Check and Checkmate

In chess, the king cannot be captured. When the king is attacked, it is known as being in check. There are three ways to get out of a check. The first is to move away from the attack, in another square that is not in the line of sight of an enemy piece. The next is to block the attack using another piece. This cannot be used when a horse is checking the king, as it can jump over other pieces. The final way is by capturing the piece that is delivering the check. When the king has no way of getting out a check, it is known as checkmate, resulting in a loss.

Prefix

    The first part of Algebraic notation is the abbreviation of the piece, in capital letters
  • King = K
  • Queen = Q
  • Rook = R
  • Knight = N
  • Bishop = B
  • Pawns have no abbreviations as they can only move in one file.

Location

The next part is the square that the piece is moving on, which is determined by the file then rank (letter in lowercase then number)

Capture

If the move is a capture, a lowercase x is put in between the piece abbreviation and the square it is moving to. For pawns, you first put the file it is on, then x, and finally the square it is moving to. For example, bxc5 is the b-pawn taking the pawn on c5.

Castling

For castling, the notation for short side castling is 0-0, and queen side is 0-0-0.

Special Cases

In some cases, the same type of piece can move to the same square. To specify, put the file that the piece is located before the square that it will move. For example, Nfd2 means that the knight located on the f-file will move to d2. If they are located on the same file, then the rank should be specified before the square it will move to. For example, N3d2means that the knight on the third rank will move to d2.

Checks and Checkmate

If the move results in a check, then a + needs to be added to the end of the notation. If the resulting check is a checkmate, then a # needs to be added.
